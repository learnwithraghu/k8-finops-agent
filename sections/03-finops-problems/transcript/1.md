# Video: Getting Started with FinOps Tagging Policy

**Time Budget:** 2 min

### Opening

You have probably reached the same conclusion we did: FinOps is the right direction. Kubernetes does not hand you a bill broken down by team — it hands you pods, PVCs, and services. If those objects are not tagged consistently, nobody can answer a simple question like *who owns this cost?*

So the natural next question is: **how do we actually get started?**

---

### Start with grouping

Before you write a single rule, you need agreement on how your organization is grouped. Who owns what? Which cost center pays for which workloads? What environments exist — dev, staging, prod? What tiers or compliance levels matter?

This is not a Kubernetes problem. It is an org problem. Platform engineers, SRE leads, and finance partners need to sit down and define the vocabulary everyone will use. Booking engine versus payment versus platform. PCI-DSS versus standard. Once those groupings exist, tagging policy has something concrete to enforce.

---

### Where do you put it?

Now you have the groupings — but where does this information live?

You have options. A **wiki page** or **spreadsheet** works fine for a human audience. People can read it, debate it, and update it in a meeting. **JSON** is great for APIs and structured output — and we will use it later when an agent *returns* findings. **TOML** and **HCL** are solid for application config. **CSV** works for simple cost-center lookups.

The question is not just "can a human read it?" It is "can automation read it reliably, and can the people who own the policy edit it without friction?"

---

### Why YAML

For a **shared contract** between humans and automation, **YAML** sits comfortably in the middle.

It is human-readable. You can add comments right next to a value — like noting that `booking-engine` owns core reservation logic. It supports lists and nested keys without ceremony. And here is the practical reason: **Kubernetes already speaks YAML.** Your deployments, services, and ConfigMaps are YAML. Your tagging policy living in the same format means one mental model — not "manifests in YAML, policy in something else."

JSON, by contrast, is a poor fit for a policy file people edit every week. No comments. Strict quoting rules. A trailing comma breaks the whole file. FinOps standards evolve — teams rename cost centers, add compliance tiers, exclude new system namespaces. Policy needs to be easy to change without a linter fight.

YAML is not trendy. It is widely accepted across the DevOps and cloud-native world — Helm charts, CI pipelines, Kubernetes manifests, Ansible playbooks. The people who maintain clusters already know it. That shared familiarity matters when you are asking multiple teams to follow the same standard.

---

### Close

So the path is: agree on your groupings, write them down in a format both humans and tools can use, and YAML is a natural choice in a Kubernetes world.

In this course, that document is `tagging-rules.yaml` in the examples folder. In the next video we will open it and walk through what is inside — the required tags, the cost centers, and how SkyLine Air maps its org structure into a file you can actually enforce.
